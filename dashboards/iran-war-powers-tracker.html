The War They Refused to Stop

Senate Republicans killed a bipartisan effort to assert congressional authority over Trump's undeclared war with Iran — and in doing so, may have drawn a permanent new line around presidential war power.

Troy W. Ferguson
March 5, 2026  ·  Data Journalism

The resolution was only a page long. It invoked a law passed in the shadow of Vietnam. And it asked Congress to do something it has not done in decades: say clearly whether the United States is at war.

S.J.Res. 104, introduced by Senator Tim Kaine of Virginia and Senator Rand Paul of Kentucky, would have directed President Trump to remove American forces from hostilities against Iran unless Congress specifically authorized their use. The resolution did not call for retreat. It did not prohibit self-defense. It asked only that the oldest constitutional prerogative of the legislative branch — the power to declare war — be honored.

The Senate answered on Wednesday with a 47–53 vote. The resolution failed. Fifty-three Republicans, with the notable exception of Paul and the equally notable defection of Pennsylvania Democrat John Fetterman, chose the president's war over the Constitution's text.

"Donald Trump has launched America into a conflict with no clear objectives, no plan, and no authorization from Congress." — Sen. Chuck Schumer

The vote landed in a country already deeply skeptical of the war it was being asked to ratify. A CNN/SSRS poll conducted just days before the vote found that 59 percent of Americans disapproved of the administration's decision to strike Iran, with 31 percent saying they strongly disapproved. Republicans backed the strikes by wide margins; Democrats and independents did not. The public, in other words, was ahead of Congress — or rather, Congress had decided the public's opinion was beside the point.

The legal architecture behind the resolution is the War Powers Resolution of 1973, a post-Vietnam statute that requires the president to notify Congress within 48 hours of introducing forces into hostilities and to withdraw those forces within 60 days unless Congress authorizes the conflict. Every president since Nixon has disputed its constitutionality while mostly complying with it anyway. Trump has done neither.

In the House, Democratic Leader Hakeem Jeffries announced his caucus would force a floor vote on H.Con.Res. 38, a companion measure introduced by Republican Representative Thomas Massie and co-sponsored by more than eighty Democrats. Speaker Johnson expressed confidence that Republicans would again defeat it. The margin, if the House indeed votes, will be narrower — and the politics considerably messier.

What the Senate produced on Wednesday was not a verdict on the war. It was a verdict on itself. Fifty-three senators looked at an unauthorized war, a skeptical public, and a Constitution that says "Congress shall have power to declare war," and chose to let someone else decide.
