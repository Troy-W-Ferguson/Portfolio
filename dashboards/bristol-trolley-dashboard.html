Methodology

Spatial Attribution of Trolley Pollution in Bristol's Waterways

This analysis begins with a historical dataset compiled by Bristol City Council documenting the number and brand attribution of shopping trolleys recovered from 18 surveyed waterways between 2005 and 2011. The dataset captures both the geographic distribution of trolley pollution across Bristol's river network and the retail provenance of each recovered unit where branding remained identifiable.

To move beyond tabular analysis, we geocoded the approximate locations of every retail outlet from the survey's store list that operated within the Bristol urban area during the sample period (2005–2011). This included current chains such as Tesco, Asda, and Sainsbury's, as well as defunct retailers — Woolworths (collapsed December 2008), Somerfield (acquired by Co-op in 2009), and Focus DIY (closed 2011) — whose trolleys continued to appear in post-closure surveys.

We then computed the Haversine distance from each geocoded store location to the nearest point on every surveyed river's centreline. Applying a 300-metre proximity threshold — representing a plausible range for trolley displacement via overland rolling, storm-drain conveyance, or deliberate dumping — we identified 12 of 33 mapped stores as "probable contributors." These riverside stores cluster heavily along the Frome corridor (Eastville retail area), the Avon New Cut (Bedminster/Southville), and Brislington Brook (Bath Road retail park), which together account for approximately 64% of all trolleys recovered across the entire survey period.

The spatial correlation is striking: the three most polluted waterways each have multiple large-format retail stores within 300 metres of their banks. The "Probable Guilty Stores" layer on the map visualises this proximity, with pulsing markers indicating stores whose trolleys had the shortest overland path to water.

Causal Inference

What Drove the Rise and Fall of Trolley Pollution?

Several converging factors explain the observed temporal patterns in the data. The overall 65% decline from 2005 to 2011 is likely attributable to a combination of industry-led deterrence and improved municipal intervention. Tesco's 89% reduction (45 → 5) is the most compelling individual case: the chain's phased rollout of coin-lock deposit mechanisms across UK stores during this period created a direct financial disincentive for trolley removal, and the data reflects this precisely.

The dramatic spike in the River Frome's count — from 8 trolleys in 2005 to 87 in 2009 — almost certainly reflects a change in survey methodology rather than a genuine pollution surge. From 2007 onward, the Frome Interceptor (a flood-relief culvert running parallel to the main channel) was included in the count. This expanded scope revealed a backlog of trolleys that had accumulated in previously unsurveyed infrastructure, creating an artificial inflation that distorts year-on-year comparison. The subsequent decline to 14 by 2011 likely represents the true rate once the legacy stock was cleared.

Brislington Brook's sustained improvement — the most consistent downward trajectory in the dataset — correlates with B&Q and Matalan's proximity to the waterway. Both retailers were within 200 metres of the brook. As B&Q's trolley count fell from 17 to 1 over the period (likely reflecting the same deposit-mechanism adoption seen at Tesco), the Brook's total declined in near-lockstep, suggesting a direct causal link between a single store's trolley-management policy and a specific waterway's pollution burden.

The rising share of unidentifiable trolleys (27% → 76% by 2010) points to a second, more troubling dynamic: the trolleys remaining in rivers by the later surveys were predominantly legacy units — deposited years earlier, their branding degraded by immersion. This "survivor bias" means that while fewer new trolleys were entering the water, the old stock was proving resistant to recovery. The 2011 dip back to 59% unidentified may reflect a concentrated clearance effort that removed some of this legacy inventory.

Finally, the appearance of "ghost trolleys" from defunct retailers — Woolworths and Somerfield units found one to two years post-closure — provides a rare empirical window into trolley residence time. These data points suggest a minimum immersion period of 12–24 months before recovery, which in turn implies that the true dumping rate during peak years was lower than headline figures suggest, as each survey captured a rolling multi-year accumulation rather than a single year's deposits.
