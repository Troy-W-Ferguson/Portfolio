Knowing Is Not Trading

What a Green Beret's Polymarket windfall reveals about the limits of the information edge

Writing Sample | Apr 29th 2026

The efficient market hypothesis rests on a seductive premise: that information, once possessed, finds its way into prices. A trader learns something; the trader bets; the price moves. Information and capital are near-perfect substitutes. The case of Master Sergeant Gannon Ken Van Dyke suggests the substitution is harder to execute than the theory implies.

Van Dyke was a senior Green Beret involved in planning Operation Absolute Resolve, the January 3rd mission that removed Venezuelan President Nicolás Maduro from power. He had classified knowledge that a geopolitically significant binary event would resolve "yes" within days.1 On Polymarket, shares in "Maduro out by January 31" were trading at seven cents apiece — a market-implied probability of 7%.2 He knew the true probability was close to certainty. He turned $33,000 into $410,000.3 Prosecutors followed, and Van Dyke pleaded not guilty in a New York federal court this week.4

The case has reignited a familiar debate. Robin Hanson, the economist who developed the theoretical foundations of prediction markets, argues insider participation is a feature, not a bug: Van Dyke's bets pushed a 7-cent contract toward its true value of $1, price discovery working as intended.5 His critics counter that ordinary bettors, finding themselves routinely on the wrong side of people who simply know the answer, will eventually stop playing.6 Both camps treat the insider as a given — a competent actor who transforms private knowledge into capital. Van Dyke is where that assumption breaks down.

Van Dyke was a bad trader. At seven cents a share, "Maduro out by January 31" offered a 93-cent edge to anyone who knew the operation would succeed. A trader with that certainty concentrates on the single contract expressing his advantage. Van Dyke placed thirteen separate bets across four overlapping contract types: US forces in Venezuela, Maduro out of power, US invasion, and Trump invoking war powers.7 It is the behavior of someone hedging against uncertainty he did not have. He diversified as though he might be wrong.

Then consider the exit. Reports of unusual activity in Maduro-related contracts began circulating in the press within hours of the operation.8 Van Dyke asked Polymarket to delete his account, claiming he had lost access to his email — a story that required prosecutors one indictment to disprove.9 He routed proceeds through a foreign cryptocurrency vault into a new brokerage account.10 A man trained to operate without a trace left a forensic trail a junior compliance officer could follow in an afternoon. The final entry: three weeks after the raid, property records showed he had purchased a house for $340,000.11 Operational security maintained across months of classified planning lasted roughly the length of a mortgage application.

The paradox cuts deeper than incompetence. Special forces selection finds people who perform under extreme stress and incomplete information. Every doctrine Van Dyke trained on optimizes for reversibility: hedge your bets, keep options open, never commit fully to a plan that circumstances may overturn. That toolkit is exactly wrong for a binary wager you know resolves in 72 hours. His training did not merely fail to help him. It was causal to his failure.

This points to something the efficient market literature tends to elide. Research on corporate insider trading finds that insiders earn abnormal returns — but the gains are modest, averaging 40 basis points per month, with a third accruing in the first month.12 Even people who genuinely know more than the market struggle to convert that knowledge into returns. Van Dyke's 12-times multiple looks exceptional only against other traders. Against what a rational actor with a 93-cent edge should have extracted, it looks like underperformance. Markets are not merely tests of information. They are tests of temperament — and Van Dyke had the wrong one.

He had a perfect hand. He played it like a man who had never held cards before, spreading chips across the table as though the outcome were still in doubt. ■
