The Russian Music Diaspora

They Fled With Their Music. Now They Play for a Country That No Longer Exists.

Since 2022, Russia’s most vital musicians have scattered from Los Angeles to Tbilisi. Three bands. Three fates. One question: can a culture survive in exile?
By Troy W. Ferguson · Berlin
Berlin — March 2026

The crowd at Forum Melbourne is mostly under thirty and almost entirely non-Russian-speaking. This does not stop them from singing. When Egor Shkutko opens his mouth and that deep, flat baritone fills the room, something below language takes over: a thousand Australians mouthing syllables in a tongue they do not understand, swaying to music that sounds like the architecture of Soviet apartment blocks rendered as synth-pop. Behind the band, a Brutalist concrete facade is projected on the screen. The Instagram bio still reads: From Minsk, Belarus. Now in LA. A homeland reduced to a prepositional clause.1

Molchat Doma left Belarus four years ago. They are part of the largest exodus of Russian-speaking musicians since the Soviet collapse, and arguably the most consequential. Since February 2022, an estimated 800,000 to 900,000 Russian citizens have left the country in what researchers call the fifth wave of Russian emigration since 1917.2 At least 650,000 remain abroad.3 Among them: dozens of the country’s most prominent musicians, rappers, and performers, scattered from Los Angeles to Tbilisi to Berlin to Tel Aviv.

Three bands chart the territory of this exile with particular clarity. Molchat Doma, the Belarusian post-punk trio, has thrived abroad, their atmospheric sound translating effortlessly across borders. Little Big, the Russian rave-pop phenomenon with billions of YouTube views, has watched its audience evaporate. And Pussy Riot, the feminist punk collective, has been formally designated an extremist organization by Moscow, its members sentenced in absentia to years in prison. Three acts. Three outcomes. Together, they trace the arc of what happens when an authoritarian state expels its own culture.

The Exiles Who Thrived

Molchat Doma formed in Minsk in 2017, three young men bonded over a shared love of minor-key synth melodies and the depressive synth-pop that had percolated through Belarus’s capital for decades. The city itself was their aesthetic: Lenin monuments, Brutalist cinemas, the Sports Palace. They self-released their debut album on Bandcamp and, in a detail that says everything about their relationship to home, they sold out venues across Europe before ever playing a full show in Belarus.4

The breakout came accidentally. In 2020, their track “Sudno,” a spare, haunting song about the Russian poet Boris Ryzhy who ended his own life at 26, went viral on TikTok, soundtracking 165 million videos, most of them comedic or goth-themed. Guitarist Roman Komogortsev was philosophical about the mismatch: “This is pure coincidence, really. We think that we have much cooler and deeper songs than ‘Sudno.’”5

That same year, the disputed Belarusian presidential election sent hundreds of thousands into the streets. Molchat Doma’s music became an unintended protest anthem. The band said nothing publicly. Bassist Pavel Kozlov later explained the calculus with bracing simplicity: “Any hasty word that was said too loud can result in a loss of freedom.”6

In late 2022, all three members relocated to Los Angeles. Vocalist Egor Shkutko was direct about the reason: they left because the war started, and they feared the borders would close.7 Their tour manager later framed it diplomatically as “a move to, not from.”8 All three left family behind in Belarus.

“Life in Los Angeles gave us more opportunities. We started working with better studio equipment, we took a different approach to sound. But the emotional core stayed the same.” — Pavel Kozlov, Molchat Doma9

Their fourth album, Belaya Polosa, released in September 2024, is widely regarded as their strongest. The title means “white stripe” in Belarusian, but it carries a second resonance: the white-red-white flag of the democratic opposition. Critics noted a tonal shift from claustrophobia to something harder to name. One reviewer captured it precisely: the music had moved from communicating “the claustrophobia of a totalitarian regime where progress feels impossible” to conveying “the melancholy of a life in exile,” where “constraints have given way to the terrifying vastness of possibility.”10

Why does exile work for Molchat Doma when it destroys others? The answer is structural. Their appeal was always sonic and atmospheric rather than lyrical. Shkutko’s voice functions, as Komogortsev once explained, “like an instrument” rather than a vehicle for narrative meaning.8 Western fans do not need to understand the words. They feel the architecture of the sound. The language barrier, for this band, is not a wall but a scrim.

The Exiles Who Faded

If Molchat Doma is the sound of Soviet concrete rendered as synth-pop, Little Big was its funhouse mirror: a punk-pop-rave act from St. Petersburg whose stock-in-trade was absurdist spectacle pushed to the point of delirium. Their track “Skibidi” became the most-viewed video in Russian YouTube history. “Uno,” their 2020 Eurovision entry, holds the record for the most-viewed video on the contest’s official channel, at 187 million views.11 By any metric of virality, they were the most globally visible Russian act of the past decade.

On March 2, 2022, six days after the invasion of Ukraine, frontman Ilya Prusikin and vocalist Sonya Tayurskaya left Russia. Someone had called Prusikin and told him to delete an anti-war Instagram post. They flew to Los Angeles that day. The government placed them on a blacklist. “We can’t make concerts, it’s impossible in Russia now,” Tayurskaya told the BBC. When asked about returning, Prusikin was blunt: “Maybe when Putin leaves, we’ll go back to Russia. But for now, it’s impossible.”12

Their anti-war single, “Generation Cancellation,” released in June 2022, was intended as a pacifist statement. It depicted war as a chess game played by old men with soldiers as disposable pieces. But some Ukrainians read the video as anti-Western rather than anti-Russian, and the band found itself in the impossible position of the exile whose protest satisfies no one.13

Then the audience evaporated. The trajectory is as steep as it is instructive. In 2024, they managed 28 tour dates. In 2025, they canceled a world tour entirely. They released only four tracks that year. By March 2026, Russian media was reporting that the band had effectively entered hiatus: “the creative resource of artists has dried up, new tracks are not shooting,” and they were accepting corporate gigs at $50,000, “significantly lower” than their previous rates. “There is still no demand.”14

The structural problem is clear. Little Big’s brand was built on a specifically Russian sense of absurdist humor: the inside joke of post-Soviet irony performed at rave tempo. Cut off from the domestic audience that understood the references, the act loses its cultural circuit. Molchat Doma’s sound travels because it is atmospheric; Little Big’s comedy does not translate the same way. Exile, for them, was not merely a geographic relocation. It severed the feedback loop that made the act work.

The Exiles Who Escalated

If the other two stories are about what exile does to art, Pussy Riot’s is about what the state does to artists who refuse to stop. Their origin story is by now canonical: the 2012 “punk prayer” inside Moscow’s Cathedral of Christ the Savior, the conviction for “hooliganism motivated by religious hatred,” the 21 months Maria Alyokhina and Nadezhda Tolokonnikova spent in Russian prisons. But what has happened since 2022 has escalated the confrontation to an entirely different register.

After the invasion, several members fled and were placed on Russia’s wanted list. In December 2022, the group released a music video called “Mama, Don’t Watch TV.” In April 2024, during a performance in Munich, one member urinated on a photograph of Vladimir Putin onstage. These are not people interested in diplomatic ambiguity.

The state responded in kind. In September 2025, a Moscow court sentenced five members in absentia to prison terms ranging from eight to thirteen years for “spreading false information about the Russian military.”15 Then, in December 2025, Russia’s Tverskoy District Court designated Pussy Riot an “extremist organization,” formally banning all its activities on Russian territory.16 Tolokonnikova grasped the implication immediately: “A balaclava under your pillow, our song on your computer, or even a ‘like’ on our post can lead to prison time. Pussy Riot has essentially become the name that cannot be spoken in Russia.”17

And yet the paradox of escalation is that it has made Pussy Riot more visible internationally, not less. In June 2025, Tolokonnikova staged a ten-day performance called “Police State” at the Museum of Contemporary Art in Los Angeles, living inside a reconstructed prison cell while ICE protests erupted outside the museum. Her anti-authoritarian art anticipated the fury in the streets of her host country.18 The Riot Days tour, which tells Alyokhina’s prison story as a hybrid of concert, theater, and political rally, has sold out venues from Stockholm to Basel in spring 2026, raising money for a Kyiv children’s hospital along the way.19 Where Little Big faded into irrelevance, Pussy Riot has converted repression into fuel.

The Scattered Scene

Molchat Doma, Little Big, and Pussy Riot are the most internationally visible faces of a much larger dispersal. Part of the Russian music business has decamped wholesale to Tbilisi, Yerevan, Helsinki, Stockholm, the Baltics, Istanbul, and Berlin. Belgrade alone hosts an estimated 200,000 young Russians who have formed their own cultural community.20 In Tbilisi, a rehearsal space called Practica serves as a meeting point for displaced musicians, a place where bands from Moscow can book studio time and play small shows for crowds made up of fellow exiles and curious Georgians.21

The roster of exile reads like the program notes of an alternative history: Oxxxymiron, Russia’s most celebrated battle rapper, now labeled a “foreign agent,” performing from London. Noize MC, the electro-rock pioneer, on a humanitarian visa in Lithuania. Monetochka, the indie-pop star who rocketed to fame on subversive lyrics, touring Europe with songs about “angst and fears about not knowing our future.” The hip-hop duo Aigel, whose Tatar frontwoman repositioned the group as a Tatar-language act to escape the stigma Western venues attached to “Russian” artists.22 The post-punk band Ploho, from Siberia, whose European gigs were canceled throughout 2022 before promoters “gradually realized that we are not terrorists or maniacs.”23 Boris Grebenshchikov, the legendary Leningrad rocker, now collaborating from London with Dave Stewart of Eurythmics.24

A festival circuit has emerged to hold this scattered constellation together. INOI, whose name is a play on inoagent, the Russian bureaucratic term for “foreign agent,” brings together acts spanning generations: Soviet-era legends alongside modern rappers, playing to Russian-speaking crowds in London, Frankfurt, and Tilburg. Monetochka, 26, described the logic: “Artists are powerful because we have the possibility to gather larger or more diverse crowds than the politicians. People love artists. Very few people love politicians.”24 Audience members at these concerts have been reported leaving in tears. They are as much group therapy as entertainment: a scattered people hearing familiar voices in rooms far from home. It is, in its own way, a Sound of Music in reverse: the Von Trapps fleeing not over the Alps but across the entire post-Soviet map, their instruments the only luggage that mattered.

But the scene is fragile. By late 2023, war fatigue had set in among both artists and their audiences. Regular album releases began overshadowing protest music. Some performers returned to Russia, unable to afford life abroad.25 Western labels told anti-war acts that any association with Russian musicians was “toxic,” regardless of their political stance.22 And the Kremlin pursued artists through diplomatic back channels, pressuring countries like the United Arab Emirates and Thailand to bar or deport anti-war performers. The rock band Bi-2 was nearly deported from Thailand after Russian diplomats intervened directly.26

The Thing They Will Not Abandon

There is one thread that runs through every exile story, from Molchat Doma’s sold-out festival stages to the cramped Tbilisi rehearsal rooms: the Russian language. Noize MC articulated it as a kind of manifesto: “It’s important not to leave the Russian language and culture to Kremlin power. We have to take it back for ourselves.”24 Monetochka said she cannot imagine achieving the same depth of expression in any language other than Russian.24

Molchat Doma provides Romanized spellings alongside Cyrillic for Western audiences. They do not translate their lyrics into English. One critic noted that this linguistic fidelity “testifies to the anxiety of dislocation”: even when the band concedes to accessibility, it refuses transparency. The words call to listeners “from far away.”10

This is the central paradox of the post-Soviet music diaspora. The language is both the artists’ limitation and their defiance. It confines their audience largely to the diaspora circuit, to the 650,000 who left and the twelve million Russian-speakers already scattered around the world. But it is also the thing that makes the music irreplaceable. To sing in English would be to concede the Kremlin’s claim that Russian culture belongs to the Russian state. To keep singing in Russian, from Berlin and Los Angeles and Tbilisi, is to insist that it does not.

Back in Melbourne, the concert ends. Shkutko steps away from the microphone. The crowd roars in English for a band that sings in Russian about a country that has, for all practical purposes, ceased to exist as they knew it. They are building something new in exile: a Russian culture that Russia itself has outlawed. Whether it can survive the exhaustion, the distance, and the slow forgetting that exile demands of everyone is the question none of them can yet answer. For now, the houses are not silent. They are playing, from very far away, as loud as they can.
