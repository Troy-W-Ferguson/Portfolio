The Unprofitable Plot

For American bosses, the true value of Davos lies in its failures

Writing Sample

For the chief executive of a Fortune 500 firm, the trip to Davos is a line item that can easily top half a million dollars once membership fees, travel, and security are tallied.1 Justifying that expense to a board fixated on quarterly returns requires a better answer than “improving the state of the world.” The official justification often rests on the promise of high-level networking and discreet dealmaking on the sidelines. But as the gap between the forum’s grand pronouncements and global reality widens, a shrewder, more contrarian case for attendance emerges. The true return on investment comes not from the deals made, but from observing the deals that inevitably fall apart.

The conventional wisdom, popular on both the left and the right, is that Davos is a conspiracy—either of plutocrats cementing their power or of globalists plotting a “Great Reset.”2 Both views flatter the attendees by assuming their competence. The reality is that the World Economic Forum is better understood as an annual, real-world demonstration of the limits of centralised planning.3 For American business, this lesson is invaluable.

Consider the challenge of a chief executive trying to build a five-year strategy. No leadership team, however data-driven, can perfectly predict the chaotic interplay of consumer tastes, supply-chain shocks, and regulatory whims. Davos is a macro-version of this corporate reality. It gathers the world’s most powerful actors to forge consensus on issues like artificial intelligence (AI) or climate policy, and their public failure to do so is the most valuable piece of market intelligence a boss can acquire.

Take AI regulation. An American tech executive might arrive hoping for a clear global framework. What they witness instead is the profound divergence between Europe’s rights-based approach, China’s state-controlled model, and America’s market-first stance.7 The “failure” of Davos to produce a single standard reveals a crucial truth: the future of AI will be a messy, fragmented, and competitive regulatory landscape.

The strategic takeaway is not to wait for harmony, but to prepare the firm to navigate discord. Similarly, listening to heads of state talk past each other on trade is a clearer signal of future geopolitical risk than any consultant’s report.

This perspective reframes Davos from a talking shop into a high-stakes stress test of globalisation itself. It provides a rare, unvarnished look at the friction points in the global system—the very points where a multinational’s strategy is most likely to break. Seeing a European regulator and an American senator disagree in person over a panel discussion on carbon pricing offers more texture and foresight than a dozen briefing notes.

For example, at Davos 2024, the Forum’s theme was “Rebuilding Trust” with an emphasis on following through on climate promises, but few concrete climate pledges emerged from the week’s sessions.9 Global CO2 emissions hit a record 37.4 billion tonnes that year—the highest ever recorded.10 A WEF-commissioned report found just 10% of companies were making meaningful progress on their own climate commitments, undermining Davos as a would-be crucible to form the world’s future order.12

Of course, the trip is not without peril. As the political winds in America blow ever more populist, the reputational risk of being branded a “Davos Man”—out of touch with domestic concerns and partial to “woke capitalism”—is growing.4, 6 This sentiment has even manifested in legislative proposals like the “Defund Davos Act” in the United States.5 The optics of discussing inequality and climate change from behind a cordon of Swiss security while breathing the CO2 fumes of nearly 1,500 private jets are, to put it mildly, unhelpful.8

For now, American bosses can still justify the junket as an efficient, if expensive, exercise in lobbying and strategic intelligence gathering. The core thesis holds: its value lies in observing the limits of elite ambition. But they should be wary. As the cost of being seen as a globalist rises in their most important market, executives may find that the most valuable insight from Davos is deciding not to go at all. ■
