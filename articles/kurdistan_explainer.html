Overview

What Is Kurdistan?

Kurdistan is not a country. It is a geographic and cultural region stretching across parts of four sovereign states: Turkey, Iraq, Iran, and Syria, home to an estimated 30 to 40 million ethnic Kurds, the world's largest stateless nation (CFR). The Kurds share a common Indo-European language, a largely Sunni Muslim identity (with significant Yazidi and Alevi minorities), and a centuries-old aspiration for self-governance that has been systematically denied by the region's dominant powers.

Today, Kurdish political life is fractured across dozens of armed factions and political parties that frequently clash with one another as much as with their host states. In Turkey, the Kurdistan Workers' Party (PKK) waged a four-decade insurgency before announcing its intent to disband in May 2025 (CFR). In Syria, the People's Protection Units (YPG), the military backbone of the Syrian Democratic Forces (SDF), served as America's primary partner against ISIS before being sidelined by the fall of Assad and the rise of the new Syrian government (Al Jazeera). In Iran, groups including PJAK, KDPI, PAK, Khabat, and Komala formally united in February 2026 as the Coalition of Political Forces of Iranian Kurdistan (CPFIK) (Wikipedia). In Iraq, the Kurdistan Regional Government (KRG) operates a semi-autonomous federal region with its own parliament, Peshmerga security forces, and oil industry, but remains locked in a bitter revenue dispute with Baghdad (HRW).

Geography & Terrain

The Zagros Wall

The Kurdish heartland is defined by the Zagros Mountains, a 1,600-kilometer chain running from southeastern Turkey to the Strait of Hormuz along Iran's western border (Wikipedia). The range's northern reaches, where Kurdistan sits, feature peaks exceeding 3,500 meters, steep interlocking valleys, and passes above 1,500 meters that have served as natural fortifications for millennia (Britannica). The Qandil Mountains, a subrange straddling the Iraq-Iran border, are the nerve center of Kurdish militant operations: peaks reach 3,587 meters at Haji Ebrahim, terrain remains snow-covered for much of the year, and the area is described as "virtually impenetrable" by military analysts (Noonpost). Roughly 5,000 PKK and allied fighters have operated in an area of approximately 50 km² here, accessible only via limited mountain roads guarded by Peshmerga checkpoints (Wikipedia).

This geography matters militarily. Iran must defend a western border stretching over 1,600 kilometers from Turkey to the Persian Gulf, with its 610,000 active troops spread across the entire country: not concentrated in the Kurdish west (Anadolu Agency/IISS). Kurdish fighters, by contrast, know every pass, cave, and ridgeline in a relatively compact zone where mechanized forces cannot maneuver and airpower is degraded by terrain masking.

Energy & Sovereignty

Oil, Autonomy & Baghdad's Leverage

The KRG sits atop enormous hydrocarbon reserves central to a decades-long power struggle with Baghdad. The KRG independently exported oil via the Kirkuk-Ceyhan pipeline until March 2023, when an ICC arbitration ruling shut it down and awarded Baghdad $1.5 billion from Turkey (Atlantic Council). The two-and-a-half-year shutdown cost Iraq over $35 billion in lost revenue and plunged the KRG into fiscal crisis, with Baghdad withholding public-sector salaries as leverage (HRW).

Exports resumed in September 2025 under an interim deal placing Baghdad's SOMO as sole marketer, producing ~220,000 bpd and generating $1.2 billion by year-end (Al Jazeera). Analysts described the deal as a "transitional truce" (Atlantic Council). In May 2025, the KRG signed separate $110 billion gas agreements with US firms HKN Energy and WesternZagros, immediately condemned by Baghdad as unconstitutional (Iraq Business News). Drone strikes on five KRG oil fields in July 2025 cut production by 70%, with officials blaming Iran-backed militias (HRW).
