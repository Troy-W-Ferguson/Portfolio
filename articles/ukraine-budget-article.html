How War Rewrote Ukraine's Budget

From a tax-funded sovereign economy to a state kept alive by allied transfers, Kyiv's fiscal transformation is without peacetime precedent in modern Europe.

By Troy W. Ferguson
March 2026  •  Cambridge, U.K.

In 2021, Ukraine paid its own bills. Its state budget general fund collected approximately UAH 1.084 trillion, roughly forty billion dollars, from a familiar mix of value-added taxes, corporate levies, customs receipts, and dividends from state-owned enterprises including the gas giant Naftogaz.(1) The International Monetary Fund was present, as it had been for decades, but only modestly so: a single Stand-by tranche of SDR 0.5 billion arrived that year, and the European Union's Macro-Financial Assistance contribution amounted to under seven hundred million euros.(2) Defense consumed less than four percent of gross domestic product. The country was neither prosperous nor fiscally adventurous, but it was, in every meaningful sense, solvent and self-reliant.

Three years later, that architecture no longer exists. Russia's full-scale invasion of February 2022 did not simply stress Ukraine's public finances. It restructured them from the ground up, replacing domestic self-sufficiency with a system of international life-support that no Western government has had to construct for a sovereign European state since the Marshall Plan. The transformation is visible in a single sentence from the Centre for Economic Strategy, the leading tracker of Ukrainian wartime fiscal data: since the invasion began, all domestic revenues have been directed to defense, and all civilian expenditures have been financed by foreign partners.(3)

The Domestic Base

What is striking about Ukraine's wartime revenue picture is not collapse but resilience. Ukrainians and Ukrainian businesses paid over one trillion hryvnias, roughly twenty-four billion dollars, in taxes in 2024.(4) Corporate income tax contributed Hr 270.8 billion. Value-added tax on imports surged as allied procurement drove goods inflows across the border. Personal income tax and the military levy together reached Hr 233.6 billion, a figure that will rise further after President Volodymyr Zelensky signed legislation raising the military tax rate from 1.5 percent to 5 percent in late 2024.(4) State-owned enterprise dividends, particularly from Naftogaz and Ukrenerhom, contributed at record levels as wartime demand for energy management inflated their reported profits.

Ukraine has also leaned aggressively on its domestic bond market. Domestic government bonds, known as OVDPs, have become the second most important source of internal financing since 2022, doubling in volume as banks, pension funds, and individual savers found they offered higher real returns than deposits.(5) By July 2025, UAH 302.9 billion in fresh domestic bond placements had been recorded in a single seven-month window alone.(6) These are not grants; they are obligations that will compound Ukraine's debt burden long into reconstruction. Public and publicly guaranteed debt reached ninety percent of GDP by the end of 2024, compared with forty-nine percent before the war.(3)

The International Architecture

The gap between what Ukraine earns and what the war costs has been bridged by an international financing architecture assembled with speed that has no postwar European precedent. The European Union's Ukraine Facility, a fifty-billion-euro envelope agreed for 2024 through 2027, allocated EUR 12.1 billion directly to the Ukrainian state budget in 2024, with a target of EUR 16 billion by year-end.(7) These funds cover salaries for teachers, doctors, and judges: the ordinary machinery of a functioning state that Kyiv can no longer pay for out of its own pocket while simultaneously sustaining a military consuming more than half the entire budget. In total, the EU and its twenty-seven member states have made available close to $197 billion in financial, military, humanitarian, and refugee assistance since the invasion began, with sixty-five percent delivered as grants or in-kind support.(8)

The United States contributed differently but at comparable scale. The U.S. Agency for International Development channeled $22.9 billion to the Government of Ukraine through World Bank trust funds prior to 2024, reimbursing Kyiv for documented eligible expenditures.(9) Deloitte and KPMG were contracted to conduct ongoing audits. In 2024, additional budget support tranches of roughly $8.5 billion followed. The IMF's four-year Extended Fund Facility, approved in March 2023 at SDR 11.6 billion, disbursed seven tranches by late 2024 at a pace calibrated to Ukraine's reform benchmarks, including anti-corruption measures, VAT alignment with EU standards, and National Bank independence.(10)

The most structurally novel instrument is the G7's Extraordinary Revenue Acceleration mechanism, which committed a $50 billion loan to Ukraine backed by the roughly $3 billion in annual interest generated by approximately 300 billion euros in frozen Russian sovereign assets held in Western custodians, predominantly Euroclear in Belgium.(11) The first tranche, EUR 18.1 billion in macro-financial assistance, began flowing in late 2024 and continued into 2025. Ukraine is expected to repay from future reparations or the eventual resolution of frozen Russian assets themselves. It is, in effect, a loan from Russia to Ukraine, intermediated by the G7.

Grain, Corridors, and the Economy Behind the War

The civilian economy, though sharply contracted, has not stopped generating foreign exchange. Ukraine's grain and oilseed exports reached 61.5 million tonnes in 2024, a twenty percent increase over the prior year, with Black Sea ports handling seventy-nine percent of total export volume despite ongoing Russian maritime harassment.(12) This export revenue does not flow directly into the state budget, but it shores up National Bank reserves, stabilizes the hryvnia, and reduces the foreign currency drain that would otherwise accelerate devaluation. The EU's Solidarity Lanes, established to replace the Black Sea Grain Corridor after Russia's withdrawal from that arrangement in July 2023, have collectively moved over 196 million tonnes of Ukrainian goods, including 88 million tonnes of grains and agricultural products, generating what Brussels calculates as critical revenue for the broader economy.(8) The metallurgical sector contributed a further $6.4 billion in export receipts in 2024, led by iron ore and semi-finished steel.(12)

Ukraine's information technology sector, while declining slightly in absolute terms due to emigration of skilled workers, still produced $6.4 billion in computer service exports in 2024, a residual of the pre-war boom that has survived partly because code crosses no physical border.(12) These private-sector flows are not budget revenue, but they matter: they fund the consumption of a working-age population that continues to pay taxes, and they provide the hard-currency base that keeps the National Bank's roughly $39 billion in reserves from eroding faster than they already are.(13)

What the diagram above makes plain is a transformation that cannot be described purely in numbers. Pre-war Ukraine was fiscally ordinary for a middle-income transition economy: largely self-funding, modestly indebted, and conditionally engaged with international lenders. Wartime Ukraine is something with few modern analogues: a state whose entire civilian apparatus is financed by allied transfers while its own citizens fund the guns. The Centre for Economic Strategy estimates that, even combining Ukraine's domestic revenues with all international assistance, the resulting total still falls $20 to $30 billion short of Russia's annual military expenditure.(14) The allies are not merely sustaining Ukraine. They are, in the most literal fiscal sense, doing so at a discount to what would be required to match the adversary on equal terms.
