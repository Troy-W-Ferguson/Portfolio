Playing with Fire

From the Great Chicago Fire to data-centre meltdowns, insurers still grapple with risks they cannot model

Writing Sample

When the Great Chicago Fire of 1871 swept through the city, 58 of 202 insurance companies facing claims went bankrupt—catastrophic events overwhelmed risk models built on predictable patterns.1 On November 28th 2025, temperatures inside CME Group’s data centre outside Chicago soared past 38°C after cooling systems failed, forcing a ten-hour shutdown of global trading in stock futures, commodities and crude oil—contracts worth over $11trn daily.2 Unlike equity markets distributed across exchanges, CME’s concentrated operations created a single point of failure.3

Insurers say historical models no longer predict the future. Under layers of actuarial data lies an uncomfortable truth: they never did. The AI systems replacing them are not fixing the problem. They are automating the delusion at scale.

The fiction of historical certainty

The industry increasingly describes disasters as “unprecedented” that are depressingly precedented. The Great Mississippi Flood has been a “1,000-year event” in 1927, 1993, 2011 and 2019. Each time, models failed. In 2024, as warming exceeded 1.5°C, insured losses surpassed $140bn—the fifth consecutive year above $100bn.8 Climate models indicate losses will worsen, yet insurers treat each hurricane season as a surprise.9 Munich Re warns of “a growing number of properties becoming uninsurable, unsaleable and ultimately unusable.”17

The exchange that could not hedge itself

The CME outage crystallises the broader failure. The exchange facilitates derivatives trading that helps businesses hedge uncertainty—yet faced a risk it could neither price nor prevent. CyrusOne, which physically manages the data centre, had reported cooling problems at 4:19am, twelve hours before markets opened.18 CME possessed a disaster-recovery plan to switch to its back-up centre in New York but chose not to, expecting a brief outage.2

The Insurer termed it “a critical vulnerability involving financial markets’ reliance on outsourced infrastructure and the insurance blind spots that come with it.”19 Analysts noted the failure was “not a black swan” but a predictable pattern rooted in cooling-system physics—the very definition of insurable risk.20 AWS, Cloudflare and nuclear-plant security systems all suffered cooling outages in 2025 alone. Yet CME’s 2016 sale of its data centre to CyrusOne for $130m, followed by a 15-year leaseback, created what managers termed a “novel risk structure”—the sort of outsourced dependency that models struggle to price.21 Outsourcing critical operations is ancient commerce. Calling it “novel” rendered it invisible to actuarial analysis.

The automation that isn’t

The AI transformation in insurance looks impressive on paper. Some 90% of surveyed insurance organisations now use AI tools, with 44% in full production.15 Lemonade, an insurtech darling, processes 55% of claims with zero human intervention; Ping An’s AI handles 1.29bn annual customer interactions.16

But look at what AI actually automates. Lemonade excels at renters insurance: small, undisputed claims averaging under $1,000, the kind that rarely test a model’s assumptions. The hard part remains untouched. Hurricanes Helene and Ian caused $6bn and $60bn in losses respectively—catastrophic events demanding human judgment at precisely the moment policyholders are most desperate and insurers most exposed.

The result is a two-tier system. Private insurers, armed with AI, hoover up profitable low-risk policies and automate their way to margins. Catastrophic risk migrates to public backstops ill-equipped to absorb it. Florida’s Citizens Insurance, the insurer of last resort, peaked at 1.4m policies as private firms retreated from hurricane-prone areas.12, 13 The National Flood Insurance Program carries $22.5bn in debt.14 AI is not solving the insurance crisis. It is sorting risk—keeping the easy bets on insurers’ books and offloading the hard ones onto taxpayers.

Three crises, one delusion

Climate volatility, technological fragility, AI’s limitations—these are not separate crises but manifestations of the same problem. Insurance has always required pricing the unpriceable. In 1871, insurers possessed 50 years of fire data; 58 still collapsed. In 2025, CME possessed decades of cooling-failure data; trading still went dark. Complicated models and AI automation are not inferior substitutes for actuarial tables—they are more honest versions of what insurance has always been: sophisticated betting on unknowables. When risk traders described CME’s outage as “flying blind,” they captured insurance’s perpetual condition. The crisis is not unprecedented risk. It is the collapse of a comfortable lie. ■
