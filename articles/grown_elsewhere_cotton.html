Grown Elsewhere

From the Piedmont to Xinjiang, the fiber has always followed the cheapest labor

Writing Sample | May 14th 2026

On May 14th, Donald Trump and Xi Jinping met at the Great Hall of the People in Beijing to discuss Chinese purchases of American agricultural goods, tariff frameworks, and the war in Iran.1 Cotton was not on the agenda. It rarely is anymore, which is itself an answer to a question the South has been avoiding for thirty years.

Brooks Brothers, founded in 1818 and self-styled dresser of 40 American presidents, filed for bankruptcy in July 2020 and shuttered its last American factories, in Massachusetts, North Carolina, and New York.2 Those facilities produced about 7% of the brand's goods. The other 93% was already made in China and Southeast Asia. The closure attracted eulogies but little analysis. It should have attracted more of the latter. Brooks Brothers did not fail because Americans stopped buying shirts. It failed because the shirts had long since stopped being American, and the cotton in them had long since stopped coming from the South.

The trajectory is worth tracing. Cotton defined the Southern economy from the antebellum period through the industrial mill era. After the Civil War, the mills arrived and replicated the extraction pattern with white labor instead of enslaved Black labor: same company towns, same fourteen-hour shifts, same logic of immobility. North Carolina alone lost 82,000 textile jobs between 1973 and 1986 as workers who had begun demanding wages that reflected their labor were replaced by workers elsewhere who had not yet done so.3 NAFTA in 1994 and China's WTO accession in 2001 provided the mechanism. The United States commanded 39% of global cotton export market share as recently as 2016.4 That figure now sits at 28% and falling. The Piedmont did not lose its cotton industry to Chinese efficiency. It lost it to a system that perfected its own historical logic.

That system is now concentrated in Xinjiang, where 90% of China's cotton grows.5 Xinjiang's yarn output rose more than 20% in 2025, fabric production climbed 36%, and 46,800 new jobs were created, figures Chinese officials announced with pride at this year's legislative sessions.6 The labor underpinning those numbers involves coercive transfers of Uyghur minorities onto cotton fields, surveillance, political indoctrination, and family separation for seasonal agricultural work. The structural parallel to the Piedmont mill system, which bound poor Southern families to company towns through debt, dependence, and the absence of alternatives, is not rhetorical flourish. It is the same economic logic dressed in different political clothes.

The Uyghur Forced Labor Prevention Act, enacted in 2022, was designed to interrupt that logic at the border. In 2025, U.S. Customs and Border Protection denied entry to textile and apparel shipments worth $55.6 million under the act, and U.S. cotton apparel imports from China fell 33%, driving China's market share below 8% for the first time in decades.6 The law succeeded as written. What it could not do was rebuild what had already been dismantled. The UFLPA worked so completely that it triggered a supply chain reorganization that permanently advantaged Brazil over the American South. The displaced sourcing did not return to the Carolinas. It went to Mato Grosso.

Brazil surpassed the United States as the world's leading cotton exporter in 2023, absorbing the Chinese sourcing the UFLPA displaced through lower production costs, higher yields, and infrastructure built with Chinese investment in Brazilian ports and logistics.4 U.S. cotton exports to China plunged 83% in 2024-25 as Chinese mills turned to suppliers in Australia and Brazil.6 The South has no mills left to fill the space the law created. As Trump and Xi spoke in Beijing about agricultural purchases, the cotton economy continued its indifferent migration: away from Xinjiang in legal terms, toward Brazil in commercial ones, away from the American South in every sense that matters.

Brooks Brothers shirts are still sold. They are still made of cotton. They are not made in North Carolina. ■
