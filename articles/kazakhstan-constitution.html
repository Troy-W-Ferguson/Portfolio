The Steppe Reclaims Its Own

Kazakhstan has voted to remake its constitution. The real story is what it is remaking itself into — and what it has quietly decided to leave behind.

Analysis

Every spring, a few weeks before the Kazakh steppe bursts into color, botanical rangers in the Tien Shan foothills conduct a quiet count. They are tallying wild tulips — Tulipa greigii, the king of tulips, native to these mountains and nowhere else on earth. The Dutch did not invent the tulip. The Russians did not bring it here. The flower originated in Kazakhstan and spread west through Ottoman gardens and into the bulb fields of Holland, not the other way around. Most of the world forgot this. Kazakhstan is in the middle of reminding it.

On Sunday, Kazakh voters approved a sweeping new constitution by an 87 percent margin, with turnout of 73 percent, a record for Kazakhstan's referendums, surpassing the 68 percent in 2022 and 64 percent in 2024. The vote was not without controversy: journalists covering the referendum were briefly detained by authorities in the run-up to and on polling day.1 The OSCE, rather than deploying a full observation mission, sent only a limited assessment team, citing concerns about transparency and the compressed timeline between the referendum's announcement and the vote.2

Putting aside these distinctly Kazakh irregularities in the voting process, the result reflects something real about the mood of a country that has spent three decades carefully separating itself from its Soviet inheritance. The new constitution streamlines parliament and recreates the office of vice president, abolished in 1996, while giving President Kassym-Jomart Tokayev the right to appoint the vice president and a host of other key officials. Critics will note, correctly, that the document concentrates power in familiar ways. But to read it only as an autocratic maneuver is to miss the larger, stranger drama playing out across the steppe.

This is also a story about language, lineage, and the slow labor of deciding who you are.

The timing is not incidental. Kazakhstan's constitutional referendum fell on March 15, the precise opening of the wild tulip season in the country's southern foothills: a bloom that predates the Dutch cultivar industry by millions of years.

The new constitution proposes making Kazakh the country's main language, while retaining Russian as another, albeit downgraded, official language. This is the quiet revolution embedded in the legal boilerplate. For three decades since independence, Russian remained the language of commerce, government, and prestige in Kazakhstan, not because Kazakhs chose it, but because Soviet policy had made Kazakh-language education nearly impossible across large swaths of the country. The language shift in the constitution is less a political act than a remediation.

The same logic applies to the tulip. During the Soviet period, Russian botanical institutes catalogued and named many of Kazakhstan's endemic species. Tulipa regelii, described and housed in the St. Petersburg Botanic Garden's herbarium, bears the name of a Russian botanist. The flower is Kazakh. The taxonomy, for a century, was Russian. The first cultivated tulips traveled to the Netherlands over 400 years ago from Turkey, where they had been brought from Central Asia. They originated in the mountainous regions and adjacent deserts of Kazakhstan more than 10 to 20 million years ago. The entire world tulip industry, from the Dutch bulb trade to the Chelsea Flower Show arrangements to the roadside petrol-station bouquets of northern Europe, is downstream of the Tien Shan. Most buyers have no idea.

Alongside structural changes, Tokayev has embedded key themes from his presidency into the political culture: a strong, legalistic state focused on social stability, secularism, environmental protection and digital progress. This brand of modernization entails a move away from Western-led liberal economic models and toward a more institutional, less personalist authoritarian system closer to the political economy of China. The Chatham House framing is apt but incomplete. Kazakhstan is not drifting toward Russia or toward the West. It is, with great deliberateness, drifting toward Kazakhstan, toward a self-conception that predates both the Soviet border and the liberal international order that replaced it.

Outside a polling station in Almaty, a 55-year-old shopkeeper named Daragul voted against the new constitution for a different reason than most opponents: it retained Russian as an official language at all. "The Kazakh language should be the only state language," she said. "We live in Kazakhstan, don't we?" Her daughter, who also voted no, worried that the document failed to address inflation and crumbling infrastructure. They disagreed about almost everything except the premise: that Kazakhstan belongs to Kazakhs, and that thirty years of independence have not yet made that fully true.

One of the main changes transforms parliament from bicameral to unicameral, renaming it the Kurultai, a word drawn from the ancient Kazakh tradition of a great assembly of tribal leaders. The name is deliberate. The Kurultai was the institution through which the steppe confederacies made collective decisions long before any Russian administrator arrived. Restoring the name does not restore the institution, and critics are right to say so. But names carry meaning. Kazakhstan is in the business of recovering its own.

None of this excuses the document's authoritarian architecture. Human rights groups have raised serious concerns. The draft places human rights within the framework of broad and vague concepts such as the "idea of a Just Kazakhstan" and the principle of "Law and Order," while the constitution would no longer contain the provision that international treaties take precedence over national laws. A human rights defender quoted by Amnesty International put it plainly: "Law becomes a substitute for rights, and Order — for freedoms." That critique deserves to stand.

But it can stand alongside another truth: that Kazakhstan's identity project is not a cynical invention of its presidency. It is something older, rooted in the red-petaled, black-blotched wildflower that carpets the Tien Shan every March, that traveled west to become the symbol of Dutch commerce, and that is only now, slowly, being named as what it always was — not an import, not a gift from another civilization, but a thing the steppe made on its own, long before anyone came to catalogue it.

The new constitution takes effect on July 1, 2026.
