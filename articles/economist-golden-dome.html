From Teapot to Golden: America’s domes of depletion

A century after one strategic reserve scandal, another is unfolding—this time in plain sight

Writing Sample

America has form when it comes to depleting strategic reserves. The Teapot Dome scandal of 1922-23 exposed how naval oil reserves in Wyoming, meant to fuel the Pacific Fleet in a war with Japan, had been secretly leased to private companies.1 Interior Secretary Albert Fall went to prison; the reserves were eventually restored. A century later, another dome faces depletion: the Pentagon’s Golden Dome, its stockpile of precision munitions. This time there is no corruption, no secret dealing. The depletion is happening in plain sight, announced in White House press releases and celebrated at military aid ceremonies.

Since 2022, the United States and its allies have transferred at least eight Patriot air-defence batteries to Ukraine along with hundreds of interceptor missiles.2 Each shipment chipped away at stocks the Pentagon had earmarked for a Taiwan contingency. Then came Iran’s missile barrage against Israel in April 2024: over 320 missiles and drones.3 American destroyers consumed air-defence interceptors at unprecedented rates.4 Defence officials now privately admit that stocks of certain critical weapons have fallen below the levels required for the Pacific theatre.

The problem is not merely depletion but production capacity. Combined American and allied production is expected to reach approximately 740 Patriot missiles in 2025, ramping to roughly 1,100 by 2027.5 Yet President Donald Trump promises his Golden Dome missile-defence shield will offer “close to 100%” protection against aerial attacks. The arithmetic is unforgiving: America would need 36,000 space-based interceptors to defeat just ten North Korean ballistic missiles, allowing for 30 seconds of decision time, according to the American Physical Society.6 Even a modest shield could cost $161bn–$542bn over 20 years.7 The arsenal of democracy now runs on just-in-time logistics, a model better suited to car parts than missiles.

Yet the contradictions multiply. For years, American presidents hectored European allies to spend more on defence. They are finally listening. European defence spending increased from €343 billion in 2024 to €381 billion in 2025.8 Washington should be celebrating. Instead, American defence contractors watch nervously as European manufacturers capture the windfall. Between mid-2022 and mid-2023, 63 percent of EU defence orders went to American companies.9 Now Europe targets 55 percent European procurement by 2030, deliberately reducing dependence on American firms.

This is where Golden Dome’s political economy becomes clear. Bipartisan caucuses in both chambers have emerged as vocal advocates for production expansion.10 Consider Huntsville, Alabama, the self-described “Rocket City” that houses the U.S. Missile Defence Agency, Lockheed Martin, and the incoming Space Force headquarters. Representative Dale Strong, who co-founded the House Golden Dome Caucus, explains that “North Alabama has played a key role in every former and current US missile defence program.” His advocacy coincides with House Armed Services Committee chairman Mike Rogers, also from Alabama, who received over $440,000 from the defence sector in the 2022 election cycle, with Lockheed Martin as his largest contributor.11

The political economy is straightforward: defence contractors donate generously and employ thousands in districts where manufacturing jobs are otherwise scarce. Lockheed Martin spent $12m on federal lobbying in 2023.10 What the caucuses describe as national security, critics call industrial policy by another name. Both are correct. The question is whether expanding domestic production for Golden Dome serves strategic needs or merely contractor profit margins.

Congress has authorised substantial funding for munitions production.12 Yet new factories take years to build, workers require training, and supply chains must be reconstructed. The munitions transferred today will not be replaced until well into the next decade. Meanwhile, Golden Dome promises to consume hundreds of billions that might otherwise rebuild depleted stocks.

Some officials invoke Ronald Reagan’s Strategic Defence Initiative, which allegedly helped bankrupt the Soviet Union by forcing an unsustainable arms race. Yet China and Russia today possess fundamentally stronger economies than the late-stage USSR. China’s GDP exceeds America’s in purchasing-power terms; Russia’s defence spending, though proportionally vast, is sustained by resource exports. Neither adversary shows signs of matching Golden Dome’s ambitions. They will simply build more missiles—far cheaper than interceptors—rendering the system obsolescent before completion. America cannot outspend adversaries who refuse to compete on Washington’s terms.

The result is strategic whiplash: America transfers munitions to Ukraine, depleting its reserves; urges European allies to spend more, which they do by buying European; and watches its own manufacturers lobby for government funding to expand capacity they cannot currently fill while simultaneously pitching an orbital interceptor constellation that physicists and economists suggest is impractical. Each sensible decision compounds the industrial shortfall.

The Teapot Dome affair required congressional investigations and criminal prosecutions to expose.1 The Golden Dome’s depletion requires only a spreadsheet. In both cases, strategic reserves were sacrificed: one for private profit, the other for alliance management and contractor lobbying. The question is whether arithmetic will impose the discipline that politics cannot. ■
